Executive Summary

Helios is exceptional at building the robot — but it is not yet built to run a connected fleet business. Overall engineering maturity is 2.6 of 5. Hardware and firmware engineering are rigorous, safety-minded, and high-craftsmanship. The modern software, cloud, and data muscle the subscription strategy depends on is underbuilt. Three systemic gaps — the inability to ship firmware fast and safely (no CI/CD or OTA), a data/cloud platform that cannot yet run a fleet at scale, and device & OT security exposure as products connect — put the connected-fleet plan at direct risk. None require heroic spend; all require system, not effort.

What's working

  • World-class hardware and firmware discipline: rigorous design reviews, safety-critical rigor, and deep embedded craftsmanship that competitors cannot easily copy.
  • A genuine product and category position in industrial/warehouse robotics, with real domain credibility and customer trust earned on reliability.
  • Leadership that asked for an independent read — the appetite to build the software side properly is already here, ahead of the crisis rather than after it.

The three systemic risks

1.6–2.0

Can't ship firmware fast or safely — no CI/CD or OTA

Firmware releases are hand-built and physically flashed; with no automated pipeline and no over-the-air update path, a connected fleet cannot be patched, improved, or secured at scale.

2.0–2.5

The data & cloud platform can't run a fleet business

Telemetry is siloed per product line and the cloud fleet-management product is immature — the platform cannot yet support the observability, billing, and analytics a subscription model requires.

2.2–2.4

Device & OT security gaps widen as products connect

Robots designed for isolated plant networks are being put online with weak device identity, secrets, and update integrity — every connected unit becomes attack surface inside a customer's operations.

Top recommendations (cross-domain)

  • Build a real embedded delivery pipeline. Stand up CI/CD for firmware and cloud and a secure OTA update path (Domains 06, 01). This is the single lever that turns a fleet of boxes into a fleet you can run, patch, and improve remotely.
  • Make the fleet data platform first-class. Unify per-product telemetry into one fleet data/observability platform that can carry billing, analytics, and subscription metrics (10, 06).
  • Secure the connected device. Establish device identity, signed updates, secrets management, and a product-security program for connected and OT-adjacent units (09).
  • Build the software-side product muscle. Stand up product management and outcome ownership for the cloud/subscription product, not just the hardware roadmap (04, 11).
  • Close the cloud/IoT talent gap. Hire and grow cloud, data, and DevOps leadership to complement deep embedded strength, and reduce key-person risk in firmware (08).

01

Software Engineering

2.9 → 3.5Maintain

Embedded craftsmanship is genuinely strong — disciplined firmware engineers who write careful, deterministic code — but the modern software tooling and flow practices the cloud product needs are thin, and delivery health is invisible because no one watches flow.

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
Code health & quality3.23.5
Delivery flow2.43.5
Engineering practices3.13.5
Technical craftsmanship & standards3.43.5
Developer experience & tooling2.43.5
Strengths
  • Deep embedded/firmware expertise; careful, review-heavy code on safety-critical paths.
  • High craftsmanship and a culture of getting it right the first time on hardware-coupled code.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Modern tooling lags: branch/build practices vary, and the cloud codebase lacks the rigor the firmware side has.
  • Hidden risk — craft without cadence: the firmware team's diligence hides the fact that idea-to-fleet lead time is unmeasured and bottlenecked on manual builds and physical flashing, so speed depends on individuals, not the system.
Recommendations
  • R01.1 Instrument flow metrics (lead time, cycle time, build/flash frequency, change-fail) across firmware and cloud.
  • R01.2 Extend the firmware team's engineering standards (review SLA, branch policy, definition of done) to the cloud codebase.

Expected impact: Lead time visible across firmware and cloud; modern practices match embedded rigor.

Strengths
  • Mature hardware milestone gates (EVT/DVT/PVT) that the whole company respects.
  • Engineers who understand disciplined, gated process and want it applied to software too.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • No real Definition of Ready on the software side; cloud scope is absorbed mid-cycle against a fixed hardware schedule.

Recommendations

  • R02.2 Stand up a capacity-aware software delivery cadence with a visible commitment line alongside the hardware milestones.

Expected impact: Software commitments as predictable as the hardware gate calendar.

Strengths

  • A quality culture that treats escaped defects as serious — a hard thing to build and already present.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — automation strong on the robot, thin on the fleet: the device is rigorously tested while the cloud, telemetry, and OTA paths it now depends on are validated mostly by hand.

06

DevOps / Infrastructure

1.9 → 4.0High

The single biggest weakness and the linchpin of the connected-fleet strategy. There is effectively no CI/CD for firmware or cloud, no over-the-air update path, and minimal fleet observability — robots are built and flashed by hand, and once shipped they cannot be updated or watched at scale.

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
CI/CD pipeline maturity1.64.0
Cloud & infrastructure-as-code2.04.0
Reliability & observability1.94.0
Incident management2.24.0
Cost management (FinOps)1.83.5
Strengths
  • Hardware build/release discipline that proves the team can run rigorous, gated processes when given a system.
  • Early cloud footprint on a modern provider to build the fleet platform on.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — a fleet that cannot be patched: with no OTA pipeline, every shipped robot is frozen at its flashed firmware, so a security fix or feature improvement requires physical access to each unit — a disqualifier for a connected, subscription-managed fleet.
  • No CI/CD for firmware or cloud; fleet observability and FinOps are absent, and releases are manual and inconsistent.
Recommendations
  • R06.1 Build CI/CD for firmware and cloud and a secure, staged OTA update path with rollback — treat this as the program's spine.
  • R06.2 Stand up fleet observability (device + cloud telemetry, alerting) and cost ownership for the growing cloud footprint.

Expected impact: Firmware shippable in days with safe OTA rollout/rollback across the entire connected fleet.

Security & Compliance

2.3 → 4.0High

Robots designed for isolated plant networks are now being connected, and the device & OT security posture has not caught up — weak device identity, unmanaged secrets, and unsigned updates turn every connected unit into attack surface inside a customer's operations.

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
Application security (secure SDLC)2.24.0
Vulnerability & patch management2.34.0
Identity & access management2.44.0
Compliance & certifications2.24.0
Incident response & resilience2.44.0
Strengths
  • Awareness at leadership that connecting the fleet changes the threat model materially.
  • Basic perimeter and cloud-provider security controls on the nascent cloud platform.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — air-gap assumptions baked into connected products: devices built assuming an isolated network ship with weak identity, hardcoded or unmanaged secrets, and no firmware-signing or update integrity, so connecting them exposes both the device and the customer's OT environment.
  • No secure-SDLC or product-security program: SAST/DAST, dependency scanning, and device threat modeling are absent.
Recommendations
  • R09.1 Establish device identity, signed firmware/OTA, and secrets management; stand up a product-security program for connected units.
  • R09.2 Embed secure-SDLC (SAST/DAST, dependency and secrets scanning), threat-model the connected fleet, and run an IR tabletop for a device-fleet compromise.

Expected impact: Connected devices have strong identity, signed updates, and a managed product-security posture.

Prioritized Roadmap

Now · Next · Later

Sequenced by impact and dependency, not by domain. The first 90 days buy predictability and unblock revenue; later phases compound on that base.

NOW · 0–90 days
Build the spine — pipeline, updateability, and the worst security gaps
  • Stand up CI/CD for firmware and cloud and a first secure OTA path with rollback (06, 01).
  • Close the worst device-security gaps — device identity, secrets, firmware signing (09).
  • Install a software Definition of Ready and intake path decoupled from the hardware gate (02).
  • Map firmware/hardware key-person risk and start knowledge transfer; begin cloud/DevOps hiring (08).
NEXT · Q2–Q3
Build the systems — fleet platform, security program, and software product muscle
  • Unify per-product telemetry into one fleet data/observability platform with cost ownership (10, 06).
  • Extend automated regression and integration testing to cloud and OTA paths; build fleet test environments (03).
  • Stand up secure-SDLC and a product-security program; threat-model the connected fleet (09).
  • Establish software/subscription product management with outcome ownership; integrated program management (04, 07).
LATER · Q4+
Optimize & compound — run the connected fleet as a business
  • Operate the fleet platform for subscription analytics, billing, and fleet-wide observability (10).
  • Classify R&D run/grow/transform across hardware/firmware/cloud and protect the connected-fleet bets (11).
  • Build out cloud/IoT career ladders, leadership bench, and onboarding for the new muscle (08).
  • Move leading domains from Defined to Managed — metrics-driven improvement across the connected stack (all).
Suggested delivery model

A focused 9-month transformation led by a fractional CTO (1–2 days/week) with two internal workstream owners — one for the embedded delivery pipeline & OTA (06/01/03), one for fleet platform & device security (10/09). Software product and portfolio moves (04/11/08) run at the leadership cadence. This is system-building, not staff augmentation: the goal is an organization that can build the robot and run the connected fleet — and sustain these gains without us.
