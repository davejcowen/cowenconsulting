Executive Summary

Meridian has a strong product and deep domain expertise — but it runs on heroics. Overall engineering maturity is 2.5 of 5. The organization can ship, but it cannot yet reliably promise. Three systemic gaps — delivery predictability, quality & release discipline, and security & compliance readiness — put the $150M growth plan at direct risk. None require heroic spend; all require system, not effort.

What's working

  • Genuine domain depth and a credible product position in building automation, energy, and IoT.
  • Engaged, capable engineers and above-average culture and retention — the hardest things to build.
  • Leadership that asked for an independent read — the appetite to fix this is already here.

The three systemic risks

2.0–2.2

Delivery is not predictable

On-time delivery ~60%. Commitments to the board and customers cannot be trusted at scale.

2.4–2.7

Quality & release run on heroics

Manual, stressful releases and untested critical paths put escaped defects on enterprise accounts.

2.1

Not enterprise/gov ready

No SOC2 and reactive security directly block the upmarket revenue the growth plan depends on.

Top recommendations (cross-domain)

  • Make delivery predictable. Install requirements-gating and a capacity-aware delivery cadence (Domains 02, 07). This single lever is how on-time delivery moves from ~60% toward 90%.
  • Shift quality left, automate release. Make critical-path tests a merge gate and build one automated pipeline (03, 06) to cut escaped defects and de-risk every release.
  • Get enterprise/gov ready. Stand up a security program to land SOC2 Type II in 9–12 months and operate controls continuously (09).
  • Manage R&D as a portfolio. Classify spend run/grow/transform, defend a strategic mix, and make engineering P&L-aware (11, 04).
  • De-risk the org. Map and reduce key-person risk and build the second line of leadership (08).

Software Engineering

2.8 → 3.5Maintain

Strong individual engineers and a healthy core product, but delivery health is invisible — no one watches flow, so quality and speed depend on individual diligence rather than the system.

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
Code health & quality3.03.5
Delivery flow2.33.5
Engineering practices3.03.5
Technical craftsmanship & standards2.83.5
Developer experience & tooling2.93.5
Strengths
  • Capable senior engineers with deep building-automation domain knowledge.
  • Code review is the norm on the flagship product; main branch is generally releasable.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • No flow metrics: lead time and cycle time are unmeasured, so erosion goes unseen.
  • Hidden risk — invisible cycle-time erosion: deploy frequency looks healthy while real idea-to-customer lead time bleeds out in review queues and a growing “don’t-touch-it” legacy core.
Recommendations
  • R01.1 Instrument DORA flow metrics (lead time, cycle time, deploy freq, change-fail) and review weekly.
  • R01.2 Define and enforce a lightweight engineering standard (review SLA, branch policy, definition of done).

Expected impact: Cycle time visible and trending down; review wait <1 day.

Engineering Process / SDLC

2.2 → 3.5High

Work enters engineering through many doors and changes mid-flight. The average delivery rate looks tolerable, but it masks a long tail of missed commitments — the single biggest threat to the growth plan.

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
Requirements & intake gating1.83.5
Planning & estimation2.23.5
Workflow & WIP management2.33.5
Release management2.43.5
Process governance & improvement2.33.5
Strengths
  • Teams run a recognizable sprint cadence with a shared tool of record.
  • A genuine appetite from EMs to tighten how work is intake-gated.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • No real Definition of Ready; unvetted scope is absorbed mid-sprint and never shows on the board.
  • Hidden risk — scope creep at the intake gate: the org looks busy and on-track right up until it blows a committed date. On-time delivery is ~60%.
Recommendations
  • R02.1 Install requirements-gating: a Definition of Ready and a single intake path before commitment.
  • R02.2 Stand up a predictable delivery cadence with capacity-aware planning and a visible commitment line.

Expected impact: On-time delivery 60% → 90% within three quarters.

Quality & Test

2.4 → 3.5Medium

DimensionNowTargetMaturity
Test strategy & coverage2.63.5
Test automation2.13.5
Defect management & economics2.33.5
Environments & test data2.53.5
Quality culture & shift-left2.53.5
Strengths
  • A QA team that knows the product and catches real issues.
  • Some unit-test automation exists on newer services.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — green coverage masking untested critical paths: aggregate coverage is high on trivial code while revenue and auth flows are barely tested automatically.
  • No automated regression gate; releases depend on manual sign-off and weekend heroics.
Recommendations
  • R03.1 Shift-left: make automated tests on changed critical paths a merge gate; track escaped-defect rate.
  • R03.2 Build a regression/perf automation suite (target 70%+ on critical flows) feeding the release pipeline.

Expected impact: Escaped Sev-1 defects −50%; release becomes a non-event.

Technical Support / CS

Escalation & engineering handoff2.93.5
Knowledge management & self-service2.53.5
Voice-of-customer loop2.63.5
Proactive support & telemetry2.83.5
Strengths
  • Strong SLAs met for enterprise accounts; knowledgeable support engineers.
  • Healthy CSAT and reasonable first-response times.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — broken voice-of-customer loop: “passed to product” is a brush-off, not a trace to the backlog, so the same pain is absorbed quarter after quarter as permanent operating cost.
  • Limited self-service / knowledge base, so deflection is low and volume scales with customers.
Recommendations
  • R05.1 Close the loop: a standing support→product triage with recurring-issue themes feeding the roadmap.
  • R05.2 Invest in self-service (KB + in-product help) and track deflection as a first-class metric.

Expected impact: Recurring ticket themes engineered away; volume decoupled from growth.

Strengths
  • Cloud footprint on a modern provider; some infrastructure-as-code in newer stacks.
  • Basic monitoring and alerting are in place.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — untested disaster recovery: no one has rebuilt production from code or restored from backup; the environment is an unreproducible snowflake until an outage proves it.

Recommendations

  • R06.2 Stand up cost observability with an accountable owner and per-service budgets.

Expected impact: Proven recoverability; predictable, owned cloud spend.

Strengths
  • Committed EMs who care about delivery and want better tooling.
  • Executive demand for predictability — a mandate to build on.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — watermelon status: projects are reported green right up to the deadline because RAG is a hand-assembled judgment call, not driven from dependency, capacity, and schedule-variance data.
  • Dependencies and cross-team risks are managed informally and surface late.
Recommendations

  • R07.2 Report delivery predictability (committed vs delivered) to leadership monthly.

Expected impact: Slips visible weeks earlier; credible executive forecasting.

Strengths
  • Awareness at the leadership level that SOC2 is now table stakes.
  • Basic perimeter controls and cloud-provider security features in use.
Gaps & hidden risk
  • Hidden risk — paper compliance: policy docs exist but controls are reconstructed in audit fire drills rather than operated continuously (MFA exceptions, standing admin access, untested backups, an unexercised IR plan).

Recommendations

  • R09.2 Embed secure-SDLC (SAST/DAST, dependency and secrets scanning) and run an IR tabletop.

Expected impact: SOC2 Type II unlocks ~$8–12M enterprise/gov pipeline.

Prioritized Roadmap

Now · Next · Later

Sequenced by impact and dependency, not by domain. The first 90 days buy predictability and unblock revenue; later phases compound on that base.

NOW · 0–90 days
Stop the bleeding — predictability and unblockers
  • Install requirements-gating + Definition of Ready (02); stand up data-driven status (07).
  • Make critical-path tests a merge gate (03); instrument DORA flow metrics (01).
  • Launch the SOC2 program and close the worst control gaps — MFA, admin access, backups (09).
  • Map key-person risk across the three sites and start knowledge transfer (08).
NEXT · Q2–Q3
Build the systems — automation and portfolio control
  • Unify CI/CD and run a DR game-day; stand up FinOps ownership (06).
  • Build the regression/perf automation suite to 70%+ on critical flows (03).
  • Classify R&D run/grow/transform and introduce ROI gating (11); outcome-based roadmap (04).
  • Quantified tech-debt register with budgeted paydown (10); close the support→product loop (05).
LATER · Q4+
Optimize & compound — measured continuous improvement
  • Reach SOC2 Type II and open enterprise/gov pipeline (09).
  • Target-state architecture + data platform for the analytics/AI roadmap (10).
  • Career ladders, leadership bench, and standardized onboarding at scale (08).
  • Move leading domains from Defined to Managed — metrics-driven improvement as routine (all).
Suggested delivery model

A focused 9-month transformation led by a fractional CTO (1–2 days/week) with two internal workstream owners — one for delivery & quality (02/03/06/07), one for security & compliance (09). Portfolio and org moves (11/08) run at the leadership cadence. This is system-building, not staff augmentation: the goal is an organization that sustains these gains without us.
